To Hell With You

March 29, 2026

This is a draft opening to a story I have in mind.

Adrian fought against the archangel Michael, buying time for his daughter to escape.

He swung his two-handed longsword and it bounced off the porcelain skin, ringing like a bell. The angel punched him in return, dropping him to his knees. Cool golden eyes studied him.

“Adrian of Erys.” It spoke in a voice that could trigger landslides. “For crimes of Treason against God / You are hereby sentenced / To eternal suffering / In Hell’s Ninth Orbit.” A halo of iridescent mana flared about its brow, the spell growing in intensity before slamming into Adrian. “[Banish].”

The world blinked. Adrian was assaulted by freezing winds and darkness. Solid ice burned against his hands and knees. A snowstorm howled in all directions.

Adrian knew he would die of exposure if he didn’t move. His sword was gone, his clothes were not fit for these conditions. Already his teeth were chattering.

He screamed, a raw animalistic sound, one of hurt and frustration and fear. I have served thee God, and I have loved thee God, and I am here God please.

The Mark of the Covenant sang in his veins, searching like an antenna for a signal. But Hell is the absence of God, and there was no reply.

Through frost-rimmed lashes he saw a faint yellow light, and stumbled towards it. The buffeting winds grew weaker as he approached, and suddenly he stepped across a threshold into preternatural calm. His breath plumed into the freezing air.

There in hushed twilight he saw the Colony for the first time. A huddled mass of gaunt and dirty sinners, faces like skulls, pressed together for warmth. Their dead eyes stared lifelessly through him.

At the center stood a naked, horned giant with a shepherd's crook. Heat rippled off his red skin and orange light bathed the congregation. They crowded pathetically towards him.

Adrian studied the scene while his fingers grew numb. He could recite chapter and verse the doctrine of samsara and the atonements demanded by the nine moons of hell. God had sent him here to suffer in the deepest pit, forever.

My daughter is still alive, he thought. I am not yet ready to die.

Grimly he jogged towards the huddled mass and pressed against it. He wrapped his arms around a group as if they were celebrating, turning his back to the freezing wind. The revenant sinners clung to each other for warmth. They stared vacantly at nothing, uncaring.

For hours he shuffled this way and that, unable to rest. He gazed up at the night sky and saw streamers of green and blue fire. Planet Earth rose above the horizon, half in shadow. White clouds swirled over blue oceans. Adrian looked at the Earth and began to cry. He cried until at some point, he fell asleep, still held in the embrace of the strangers in the Colony.

Adrian woke in predawn light to the stench of unwashed bodies. The storm had broken during the night, leaving clear pale skies. Naked ice stretched to every horizon.

He untangled himself from the mass and went to piss out in the open. The demon watched silently.

There were bedrolls and even tents out here at the colony’s perimeter. A dozen or so men with packs and gear were preparing to head south.

“Where are you going?” He called hoarsely. They did not reply. He found it difficult to care. Instead, he dropped to his knees and prayed.

“Lord, help me. I am yours to command. My heart is yours, my speech is yours, my sword is yours. Please, Lord. I trust that you are working in ways beyond my sight. Please forgive me. Please do not… please, give me a sign.”

He was not the only one praying. There were dozens of ragged beggars mumbling their pleas to the silent sky. There was nothing else to do.

Hours later he noticed the men returning from the south. They carried wicker baskets of fish on their backs and seemed in good spirits. One by one they visited the demon, kicking aside any wretch in their path. Each man paid a tithe of fish to the demon, and received a blessing in return. Adrian watched them thoughtfully.

After the fishermen left, the demon passed his hands over the offerings, and it seemed that one fish became three. Thus he multiplied the fishes before the beggars, and invited them to feast. The emaciated sinners ate the fish raw.

Adrian crowded with the beggars at sunset. The demon shone like a topaz lantern, his magic shielding the flock from the cold. Adrian rested his head on a stranger's shoulder, and dreamed.

He was back in Erys, just returned from the war. Rebecca lay in her bed, still fighting the illness that would ultimately consume her. The fever was worsening. She was getting weaker by the day.

“I can come with you,” he whispered.

She opened her eyes and smiled. “I can wait for you in the next life.” She had to stop and catch her breath. “You need to look after Aesha.”

He felt urgently that he needed to tell her something, something that he couldn’t remember. “I will look after her,” he promised. “I will keep her safe.”

I will keep her safe.

The dream fractured and dissolved. Adrian was standing in the host of sinners. He opened his eyes and looked up at the glowing red demon, steam billowing off its shoulders, framed by twinkling stars.

He sought out the fishermen at first light.

He still wore the clothes he’d been banished in - a cloth tunic, linen pants, cloak and shoes. Good for a cool night, but useless against these negative temperatures. The soles of his shoes were freezing cold.

His target watched him approach. The man wore leather parka and trousers with a fur-lined hood. Gloves hid his hands. His boots went up to mid-thigh. Only his face was exposed to the wind.

“I’m Isaac,” said the man. “Who the fuck are you?”

"Adrian of Erys. Can you lend me clothes?”

Isaac rested his hands on the daggers at his belt. “Why should I give you anything but a knife in the ribs?”

Adrian squared his shoulders. “I have commanded armies in battle. I have slain enemies stronger than any of the mongrels here. Not for long will I lie with the dogs, Isaac.”

The man gave him a mirthless sneer. “Should I be impressed?” He rummaged in his bedroll and tossed some things to Adrian. “Here. Just remember the hand that fed you, cur.”

Adrian quickly donned the castoffs. “Who leads the fishing parties?”

“You want to talk to Samson.”

The yurt he pointed to was larger than the others. Samson himself was heavyset, with sunburn on his face and a streak of white in his black hair.

“I want to join you today,” Adrian said.

“Nice to meet you too. Do you know how to fish?”

“Fish, hunt, fight.” Adrian shrugged. “Can I come?”

“Sure. It’s about five miles—that way.” Samson swung his pack on his shoulders. “We won’t slow down if you can’t keep up.”

“Fine.”

They set a hard pace across the ice. After an hour the ice sheet gave way to volcanic basalt and black shingle. The trail led between sculpted rock outcrops to a sheltered bay with a black pebble beach. A single rowboat was moored to the pier.

Samson stopped them next to three boulders of increasing size.

“Lift a stone to determine your share,” he said. He touched each boulder in turn. “Half share, full share.” He slapped the last one. It was as big as a washtub. “Double share if you can lift the fullsterkur.”

Adrian stripped shirtless while the men dropped their loads. He went straight to the fullsterkur stone. It was a misshapen egg and must have weighed four hundred pounds. He dragged it up onto one side and squatted by it, hugged it, and slowly lifted it until it rested in the crease of his hips. He paused there for a second, wrapping his arms tightly around the awkward shape, before thrusting and hoisting it to the shoulder. Samson and the others stared at him in surprise.

He wasn’t finished yet. Adrian grappled with the rock, moving his hands to the right position, then pressed it up over his head, trembling with the effort.

The black pebbles crunched as he dropped the stone. “Double share, then?”

Samson scowled. “Sure. We’ve got one too many people, though.” He paused. “If you kill Isaac then you can take his place.”

Isaac drew his daggers, eyes glittering. Rocks skittered away as he dropped into a fighter’s crouch.

“I don’t feel like killing a man today,” said Adrian slowly.

“Then you’re staying here,” said Samson. “Men, with me.” He turned on his heel and strode away.

Isaac’s eyes searched Adrian's, then he sheathed his daggers and followed.

Adrian watched them load the boat and depart into the wine-dark sea.

He waited a while, listening to the screams of the sea birds on the cliff. Eventually he went to the treacherous rocks and began to climb. The birds shrieked in outrage as he collected their white speckled eggs.

He left for the Colony before the boat returned.
